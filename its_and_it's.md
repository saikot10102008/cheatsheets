The difference between **its** and **it's** is one of the most common mix-ups in the English language, but it is actually very easy to master once you know the secret.

The trick is to remember that **the apostrophe is a placeholder for a missing letter**, not a sign of ownership.

---

## 1. When to use **It's** (With an Apostrophe)

**It's** is a contraction (a shortcut) for two words: **it is** or **it has**.

> **The Golden Test:** If you can replace the word with "it is" or "it has" and the sentence still makes sense, use **it's**.

* **It's** raining outside. *(It **is** raining outside.)*
* **It's** been a long day. *(It **has** been a long day.)*
* I think **it's** a great idea. *(I think it **is** a great idea.)*

---

## 2. When to use **Its** (No Apostrophe)

**Its** shows ownership (possession). It means "belonging to it."

Even though we usually add an apostrophe plus an *s* to show ownership for people (like *Sarah's car* or *the dog's bone*), possessive pronouns like *his, hers, yours,* and **its** never use apostrophes.

* The dog wagged **its** tail. *(The tail belongs to the dog.)*
* The hotel raised **its** prices. *(The prices belong to the hotel.)*
* The smartphone lost **its** charge. *(The charge belongs to the phone.)*

---

## The Quick Test Summary

Whenever you are unsure, read your sentence out loud and say **"it is"** instead of the word.

* "The cat licked **it is** paw." $\rightarrow$ **Does not make sense.** Use **its**.
* "**It is** a beautiful morning." $\rightarrow$ **Makes perfect sense.** Use **it's**.
