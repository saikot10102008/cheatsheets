While **has** and **have** are used for the present tense, **had** is your go-to word for the **past tense**.

The best thing about **had** is that it is much easier to use than *have* or *has* because **the rule never changes based on the subject.**

---

## 1. The Universal Past Tense

Whether the subject is singular, plural, *I*, *you*, *he*, or *they*, you **always use had** when talking about the past.

* **I** **had** a bicycle when I was a kid.
* **She** **had** a cold last week.
* **They** **had** a meeting this morning.
* **The dogs** **had** fun at the park yesterday.

---

## 2. Past Actions That Happened Before Another Past Action

In grammar, this is called the **Past Perfect tense**. You use **had** alongside another verb (like *eaten, left, started*) to show that one thing happened *before* something else in the past.

* "The train **had** already **left** when I arrived at the station."
*(First the train left, then I arrived.)*
* "I realized I **had forgotten** my phone at home."
*(First I forgot the phone, then later I realized it.)*

---

## 3. Double "Had" (*Had Had*)

Sometimes you will see **had had** right next to each other in a sentence. It looks like a typo, but it is actually grammatically correct!

This happens when you are using the Past Perfect rule from above, and the main action verb just happens to be "to have" (meaning to possess, eat, or experience).

* "I **had had** that car for ten years before it finally broke down."
* "She **had had** breakfast already, so she wasn't hungry at lunch."

---

## 4. Questions and Negatives in the Past

Just like in the present tense, when you ask a question or make a sentence negative in the past, a helper verb (*did/didn't*) steps in. When **did** or **didn't** is in the sentence, **had changes back to *have***.

* **Positive:** She **had** a test.
* **Negative:** She **didn't have** a test. *(NOT: She didn't had)*
* **Question:** **Did** she **have** a test? *(NOT: Did she had)*
